**Take 1.** 
Pushing through the throng of suit jackets, the smell of sweat haunted by  stale aftershave. The bar was crowded. A typical Dubai drinking hole for  advertising and film people. They called it the Red Lion, pretending that it  was the same as you find in England. Except it wasn’t. 
Like the city, it was faux, built on dreams and hopes, rising from dry dusty  sand. Like a film set. Glittering on the inside, hollow on the inside It was my escape, this Las Vegas in the Middle East, a haven for a film  director. Being busy, pushing for the next job, networking, was the way to  fill life with money and meaning. From the hollowness of nothing.  But that realization came later . When I saw depression. Not felt it… saw  it… like a glowering creature that enveloped my body with sadness, a fear  that, if this was all life was about, then it was not worth it. It saw me, the  real me, naked. 
The beach was my own getaway, my escape from the pressure of  pretense. Sinking beneath the water was a meeting with me without  judgement. It was often my only salvation. 
Tonight though, under the nicotine coloured ceiling, I played at being  buoyant, enjoying the noise of revels, inhaling the camaraderie of the film  crew who smiled and offered me a double J@B scotch on the rocks. Any  noise. Until there was none. Like the pauses when they did not know what  to say to me. 
Film crews, like theatre folk, believe the dream they are living. On  location, away from family, kids and school trips, they hold onto each  other, supporting the excitement of ‘Action, Roll Camera’ life until the  next take. 
And then after the grueling day in the desert near Sharjah, in heat of forty  degrees, they bond with alcohol and stories of the day.
‘Did you see how we nearly lost the camera in that shot of the Land  Cruiser sliding down the sand dune.’ 
‘Ja…I thought we were going to lose the light.’ 
‘Did the director know what he was doing?’ 
The driver of the car wasn’t there to tell his side of the story; how I had  insisted he breast the sand dune at high speed so that I could get a shot of  the car in the air over the sand. 
‘Cheers!’ 
‘Terrific day, Sir,’ the cameraman said. Hard eyes, no smile. ‘Here’s your  Scotch!’ I downed it in one gulp. I was exhausted from the day’s  concentration and the tension of being the director- the man in charge of  the shoot. It is all an act- I must appear to be in charge, that all questions  can be answered by me. Major imposter syndrome. They tell you that the  best directors choose the most experienced crew to lessen the pressure. I  found the reverse. Better people expect better knowledge from the man in  charge. And I did not know enough. Constant questions which bludgeoned  my creative vision. 
‘What lens do you want, sir?’ 
‘Where do you think we should put the light. Sir?’ 
‘Don’t you think we need to have the car tyres blackened, sir?’ ‘How long do you think it’ll take to clean the sand of wheel marks before  the next take, sir?’ 
‘What time do you want to break for lunch, sir?’ 
‘Can we see the driver in the car in this shot, or do we use a double, sir?’ ‘Time is running short. We will miss the sunset shot, sir.’ 
‘I’ll have another one, ‘I said. The barman nodded and I downed the  scotch.
The barman was used to film crews who over worked and overdrank. He  smiled, handed me my change and I turned to watch the crew, most  showing the effect of their drinks. They included me but not as one of  their own. Directors were the people who were papal if they were  considered good at their job, but unforgiven if they failed to meet the  crews’ expectations. On this shoot I sensed a disdain behind their smiles. I  sighed. The scales were falling off, I was beginning to hate this business,  my ability to play the game. Perhaps I was just tired. 
‘Reduced crew tomorrow,’ said the assistant director. 
‘You handle it, ‘I said. ‘It’s just postcard shots of dunes and thorn trees. I’ll  begin the edit.’ 
He looked chuffed to be given the responsibility. 
‘I’ll do my best, sir.’ 
He assumed a straighter posture when he told the crew. 
‘The wanker said I will take the shoot tomorrow,’ he said.  Well, that’s what I would have said. 
I pushed through the sweat and cigarette smoke, the retelling of the land  cruiser flying over the sand dune; again and again with every drink. I left. 
The street was quiet outside the noise of the pub. It felt safe, away from  the pressure of people drinking to enjoy themselves. I was empty. Double  scotches had not filled the gap. These crew members weren’t friends; they  were, at best, colleagues. But the sinking thought, the realization that I did  not respect them, that they certainly did not respect me, hit me like the  humidity fogging up my glasses. I was drowning. What was I looking for?  That they should like me? That they should be impressed by my artistic  skill? I had no answers. I did not want these thoughts on a humid street in  Dubai.
I hailed a cab and headed for a bar in Satwa, where local Emiratis drank  and picked up prostitutes. 


**Take Two**
It was a fug of smoke and the feeling of being an outsider was comforting.  No one knew me, had any expectations.The light was low, yellow and  smokey. 
Thobes and gutras thronged the bar. Ethiopian and Moroccan women  perched on stools glad-eying the men. 
‘Need a friend?’ said one to me as I finished my first drink. ‘La, habibti,’ I said. I was not in the market for sins of her flesh. The crowd was dense, and I caught a young dark man looking at me. Not  visible at first, hidden behind customers and smoke, I caught him staring  again. His face came in and out of the light, as if he was dodging a  spotlight. When I got a full view, I thought he looked familiar, but I did not  recognize or know him. Like a dusty reflection from a mirror. I peered to  see if perhaps he was with people I knew, but he was alone. Then, as I  stared at him, he nodded, as if I had given him permission, and walked  over slowly. Carefully. 
‘I’m a student,’ he said. 
I laughed.  
‘Sure,’ I said, ‘And you want money for love.’ 
‘Like you,’ he said. I turned away. I did not need bar philosophy. Who was  he anyway? 
‘I saw you filming the car on the sand dune. I was part of the crew to clean  the car after every shot.’ 
I frowned at him. A man from my film shoot, emerging out of a cloud of  cigarette smoke, in a bar my normal crew would never visit. He was  unnerving, I thought I should know him. As if he was familiar. ‘Only brown people can clean cars on film sets,’ he said, without irony. He was right. No white people were used as labour on films. I sighed and  downed my drink. I could not get away from my career, even in this dank  bar. 
‘You are good at your job,’ he said. 
‘Must be paying you too much if you’re out drinking!’ I said. He laughed. ‘I don’t drink. Don’t have the need.’ 
His voice was deep and warm. Not like the voices of labourers who  gabbled, as if the next moment would be their last. This man spoke  without accent. At times I thought he and I had the same voice. He was a handsome man, in a Bollywood way, sweeping eyelashes and  dark long hair. He reminded me of the actor from a Monkey King series I  had watched on TV. Why was he here? 
‘Never seen you here before,’ I said. 
‘I needed company.’ 
I shrugged. 
‘You’ve got it here.’ I said. 
‘Like watching from a distance.’ 
‘Yeah…like watching from a distance…’ 
’Why this bar?’ 
He smiled. ‘If I go to a Western bar, they treat me like a waiter. Just like on  the film set.’ 
‘Drink?’ I offered. ‘Coke, or 7 UP?’ He shook his head. ‘I’ve had my fill,’ he  said. We stood at the bar, leaning against the counter, watching the ladies  chat up the men, some leaving with customers, others patiently smoking. ‘Why here? ‘I said again. 
‘Why not?’ 
Normally labourer types don’t go into bars and talk to white men. He  spoke to me as if we had met before. Like a memory. There was something strange yet intimate about him as if we were in our own searchlight at the  bar. 
‘And you? You’re the only westerner here.’ He said. 
He was right, the only white man in the room. 
‘An obvious client for the ladies.’ We laughed. 
‘I’m not here for that.’ I said. 
‘For what then?’ 
I had no answer for this stranger, sharing my space. But I remained silent.  He would not know tonight was one of my worst times, doubting the  person, whether behind the mask was real. Perhaps he did, which he is  why he came over. 
‘You are very patient on the film set,’ he said. 
‘I don’t often feel it.’ 
‘I saw that. You work with people you don’t like. Who don’t like you. But  you do the job.’ 
‘That’s what you have to do,’ I said. 
‘Bare spots and darkness.’ 
‘What?’ 
‘Walt Whitman. He said an artist must know lows and highs.’ ‘I need another drink after that,’ I said. He smiled. 
‘Poetry and prostitutes…’ 
Was this man real? I turned my back on him and scanned the room. Noise  and moving mouths. 
‘I’m going to the beach’ he said eventually. 
‘Bit late for a swim.’ 
‘No swimming. I get peace there.’ 
‘I live on the beach.’ I said.

‘I know. I was at your wrap party.’ 
‘For the last film?’? 
‘Yes. But in your job, you don’t see people.’ 
I felt stung. 
‘I’ll see you tomorrow,’ he said. 
‘Not me-second unit. I’m not necessary.’ 
He turned and after a pause. ‘Your river. It’s your bridge you are crossing.’ It should have sounded strange, but I concentrated on my drink. I must  have misheard him. 
He had left by the time a new scotch arrived and the heat of the whisky,  the promise of rest, eased me as I stared at the crowd; a tourist examining  items in a window display he could or would not buy. 
‘Double Scotch,’ I said to the bartender.


**Take Three**
The sand crunched though my toes as I stumbled towards the sea. The air  was heavy with moisture, blanketing my skin in sweat. 
The waves rippled along like a ribbon, white lights of foam suddenly  spreading across the sand. In the distance, through the humid haze, the  lights of the soaring Burj Khalifa reached to the sky. The desire to have the  tallest, the biggest, the fastest; the signature of Dubai. Using cheap labour  from India and Iran, the city is an example of materialism at its best and  worst. When I first arrived here, Indian labourers were forced to work  during the hottest period of the day, some falling to their death from the  scaffolding. Family hopes gone. 
Nowadays the hours are proscribed. But still they fall. Now into a safety  net. 
I swigged from the bottle of whisky I had bought at the bar. I looked back  at my cottage, more of a lean-to, overlooking an undeveloped part of the  beach front. Built in the seventies as houses for migrant labour to build  the port, they were now occupied by artists and freelance film makers,  like me. 
The rent was a pittance, the cottages were basic, built not to survive, but  they did, and we took them over. 
I loved living there. I could control my expenses and choose which jobs I  wanted, without money pressures. But the question would not go away.  What would happen if I gave it up? I had no life line. 
I began to strip. I would swim. The water lapped against a half constructed  bridge which would eventually ruin the beach. Soon my sanctuary would  be a carpark; my haven would be gone. 
I left the bottle of whiskey on top of my clothes to stop them rolling away in the desert wind. 
I stumbled to the edge of the sand holding the shadow of the ebbing tide.  Naked in the humidity was a release and I edged into the water. It was  lukewarm from the heat of the day. As I waded out, I wondered would I  have the strength to drown. Surrender or swim. Shoulder deep, I started  to relax. 
It was then I felt fear, it gripped me. I felt an orange glow, threatening to  push me under, overwhelming me, like dread, silent screams around me, suffocating me. Trickles of pain travelled through my arms, my feet, my  face. I panicked. It must be the alcohol. Panting, I grasped bare concrete and stone. A pillar of the bridge. I clawed on to it until my breathing  settled. I looked around at what had threatened me. 
Nothing.
I released my grasp of the pillar slowly and turned to the shore. An attack  of terror in the water at night. That was all it was. 
I dipped under, feeling the brief sting of salt in my eyes. Slowly I swam,  then stumbled back to the shore. 
In front of me was a lone figure, sitting cross legged, staring out at the  ocean. Cast in faint shadows and light. The young man from the bar. Was he following me? Why was he there? I felt I needed someone….like a  witness to my mind. So I stood beside him. To share our aloneness. He did  not move.  
I had nothing to say. It was still now, no breeze. Nothing. Just this man. My  shadow crossed over him. By sharing his isolation I was destroying  it. Drunken thoughts from drinking too much at the bar. 
Fear of leaving the comfort of anonymous people in search of sex and  booze, of stepping into a real world, fueled my thirst. Strength so I could  leave. 
The drink relaxed, stopped me pretending to be normal. It was exhausting  to hide the trembles of the person behind the mask. 
I sat down on the sand , cross legged and tried to work out what the man  was staring at. Eyes straight ahead, looking beyond the boats. A light from  the distance passed over us, like a lighthouse. There one minute, gone the  next. Like a memory. 
He did not acknowledge me or react to my nakedness. Most Middle East  people would avert their eyes from nudity. He did not even notice. He was  dressed in jeans and a loose shirt with sandals neatly arranged near his  feet. 
Complete, this man had punctured my thoughts,had roiled me. Made me  envious. We sat in silence. My mind was not. Whether he felt it or not, I  cannot say.  
‘That is my home,’ he said. 
I looked out over the black waves, every now and then punctuated by the  lights of ships queueing for the harbour.
‘The ships?’ 
‘My country.’ 
‘I can’t see anything,’ I said. 
‘You will if you try.’ 
‘Iran is across the sea from here,’ I said. 
He turned to me and smiled. A gentle smile.’It’s hundreds of miles away,’ I  added. ‘You can’t see it.’ 
‘It’s still there.’ He said. 
The waves plashed along the shore, lights flicking, then gone, boats hazy  in the distance. 
I offered him a swig from my bottle. He shook his head. 
‘I’ve had my fill,’ he said again. 
‘So have I.’ 
We sat in silence, slowly watching the sky lighten.  
‘You should dress.’ He said.’You must choose who sees you naked.’ Nudity on the beaches was not allowed. 
I grinned as I slipped on my clothes. ‘The secret police will think we are  lovers.’ 
‘In a way we are,’ he said. I remember frowning at him. I thought to put my  hand on his shoulder but paused. ‘I was looking for a friend too.’ I turned to trudge up to my house in the grey dawn. 
When I looked back, he was not there.
